Surfacing relationships in the National Theatre Archive

An exploratory data-humanities analysis of 937 National Theatre productions (1963–2028) drawn from four Calm XML exports. We join Performance records to the full Role Export to reconstruct the hidden network of collaborators — directors, writers, performers, stage-management teams and creative staff — and answer the three questions posed in the project briefing.

The current CalmView public interface lets a visitor look up a single production, name, or role, but it doesn’t expose the links between them. By joining PerformerRole / CreativeRole references in each Performance record to the full Role Export we are able to walk those links explicitly, assemble a collaboration graph, and answer concrete research questions.

Headline findings
  • Q1 · director × writer Peter Hall directed 11 Shakespeare productions in this dataset — the most repeated director-writer partnership in the archive. Nicholas Hytner follows with 8 Shakespeare productions.
  • Q2 · actor × director Daniel Thorndike worked with Peter Hall on 10 productions — the most repeated actor-director pairing. Simon Russell Beale–Sam Mendes is the modern equivalent (6 productions).
  • Q3 · stage management Lee Harrington is credited on 31 productions under stage-management titles — more than any other stage-management team member in the 2010s corpus.

2. Data sources & pipeline

Four XML files were supplied by the NT Archive (a Calm cataloguing export). We parse them with lxml and normalise into four tidy CSV tables — productions.csv, roles.csv, credits.csv, and people.csv — stored alongside this report for reuse.

After parsing and deduplication we obtain 937 unique productions, 23,345 credit rows (23,169 linked to a person record), and 10,709 distinct individuals. Of these productions, 466 have at least one named director in the Role Export.

11. Methodology, caveats & recommendations for the NT Archive

Method

  1. Parse each performance XML with lxml. Each <DScribeRecord> corresponds to one production; its <PerformerRole> / <CreativeRole> children contain role codes (ROLE…), not names.
  2. Parse the Role Export separately and build a dictionary RoleCode → (job title, person, NameCode).
  3. Join the two to materialise credits.csv: one row per production × role, enriched with the person and job title.
  4. Build pairwise collaboration counts (director↔writer, actor↔director, director↔anyone) and then NetworkX graphs. Visualise static views with Matplotlib and interactive views with PyVis.

Data caveats

  • Not every performance record has a director linked. Only 466 of 937 productions carry at least one Director role — chiefly because some early or special-format events (platforms, talks, readings) don't use that job title.
  • Name strings contain honorifics, life-years and reversed “Last; First” order. We normalise to “First Last” for analysis but keep raw fields for traceability (name_raw).
  • A handful of performance records use group credits (e.g. “Members of the Company”) rather than individual actors, inflating those as top performers. Worth flagging for the Archive team.
  • Performer job-title hygiene: many performer rows carry descriptive character names (e.g. “Citizen of Tyre…”) rather than generic “Cast”. Our actor counts therefore rely on credit_type = Performer (from the <PerformerRole> tag) rather than job_title.

Recommendations for the NT Archive

  • Expose relationships in CalmView. The data is already there — just hidden behind link tables. Even a simple “people on this production” panel, or a “productions with this person” reverse-lookup, would unlock most of the questions this project answers.
  • Adopt a controlled vocabulary for stage-management titles. We identified 20+ variant spellings (“Assistant Stage Manager (Olivier)”, “Placement Assistant Stage Manager”, etc.). Canonicalising would make career tracking trivial.
  • Consider a public, read-only API or CSV download. This entire report was built from the supplied XML in under 30 seconds of compute — researchers would benefit hugely from that data being re-usable at will.
  • Opportunity: network-based discovery. The collaboration graph is a natural discovery tool (“show me other productions involving Simon Russell Beale’s regular lighting designer”), especially for researchers and press.
